# Early Britain
## Stone Age
- first people were hunter-gatherers in the Stone Age
- Britain was connected to Europe by a land bridge
- people flowed in/out following animals (e.g., deer, horses) they hunted
- **10,000 y.ago**: Britain got separated from Europe
- **6,000 y.ago**: first farmers arrived from SE Europe (probably)
- They built houses, tombs, monuments
    - **Stonehenge** in **Wiltshire**
    - **Skara Brae** in **Orkney** (N coast of Scotland) <- best preserved
      pre-historic village in N Europe; shows late Stone Age life
## Bronze Age
- **4,000 y.ago**: bronze age
- people lived in round houses and buried dead in round tombs "round barrows"
- accomplished metal workers: ornaments, tools and weapons
## Iron Age
- made tools, weapons with iron
- lived in round houses, larger settlements, built hill forts
  - **Maiden Castle** in **Dorset**
- language spoken: **Celtic** family
  - similar language spoken across Europe
  - lineage still spoken in Wales, Scotland and Ireland
- sophisticated culture and economics
  - first coins minted in Britain (inscribed with names of the kings)
  - marks the beginning of British history
## Romans
- **55 BC**: **Julius Caesar** - failed attempt to invade Britain
- **43 AD**: **Claudius** - successful invasion
  - some tribes resisted
    - **Boudicca**, the queen of **Iceni** (E England)
      - statue on **Westminster Bridge** near the **Houses of Parliament**
- occupied almost all of Britain
- Scotland was never conquered
- **Hadrian's wall**
  - to keep **Picts** (ancestors of Scotland) out
  - had a number of forts:
    - such as in **Housesteads** and **Vindolanda**
    - world heritage site by **UNESCO**
- **410 AD**: The Romans left to defend other parts of the Roman empire
- contributions:
  - built roads, public buildings
  - created a structure of law
  - introduced new plants and animals
  - **3rd and 4th century**: first christian communities appeared
## Anglo-Saxons
- invaders from N Europe: **Jutes**, **Angles**, **Saxons**
- basis of English language
- **600 AD**: Anglo-Saxon kingdoms established, mainly in England
- **Sutton Hoo** in **Suffolk**: burial of one of the kings
  - buried with treasure and armour
  - placed in a ship
  - covered by a mound of mud
- part of W Britain, including Wales and Scotland remained free of
  Anglo-Saxon rule
- Christianity:
  - Anglo-Saxons were not Christians at first
  - Missionaries came to Britain to preach Christianity
  - Missionaries from Ireland spread Christianity to the N
    - **St Patrick**: patron St of Ireland
    - **St Columba**: founded a monastery on the island of **Iona**, off
      the coast of Scotland
    - **St Augustine**:
      - led missionaries from Rome
      - spread Christianity to the S
      - became the first Archbishop of **Canterbury**
## Vikings
- **789 AD**: vikings first raided coastal towns
  - to take away goods and slaves
- came from **Denmark** and **Norway**
- began to stay in E England and Scotland
- King **Alfred the Great**:
  - united the Anglo-Saxon kingdoms in England
  - defeated the Vikings
- **Danelaw**: many Viking invaders stayed in Britain, esp. E and N England
  - Names of many places there come from Viking languages
    - **Grimsby**
    - **Scunthorpe**
- Vikings mixed with local community and converted to Christianity
- Anglo-Saxon kings continued to rule England
  - except for a short period when there were Danish kings
    - **Cnut** / **Canute**: the first of these kings
- **Kenneth McAlpin**: in the N, Viking attacks encouraged unity under this king
- Scotland: the name started to be used
## Norman Conquest
- **1066** — **Battle of Hastings**: **William**, **Duke of Normandy**, invaded
  England, defeating **Harold**, the Saxon king of England
  - Harold was killed
- **William**:
  - was known as **William the Conqueror** and became the king of England
  - **Bayeux Tapestry**: a great piece of embroidery commemorating the battle
    of Hastings (can still be seen in France)
- Norman conquest:
  - the last successful foreign invasion of England
  - brought many changes in govt and social structures in England
  - **Norman French**:
    - the language of the ruling class
    - influenced the development of English language
- Wales:
  - was initially conquered by the **Normans**
  - but the Welsh gradually won the territory back
- Scotland:
  - Scots and Normans fought many battles at the border (England / Scotland)
  - took some land on the border
  - but never invaded Scotland
- **Domesday Book**: William surveyed list of towns and villages
  - included:
    - people
    - land ownership
    - animals owned
  - still exists today
  - depicts society in England just after Norman Conquest
