# A Global Power

## Constitutional monarchy / Bill of Rights
- after the **Glorious Revolution**, the balance of power shifted permanently
  from the monarch to **Parliament**
- at the coronation of **William and Mary**, a **Declaration of Rights** was read
  - the king could no longer raise taxes or administer justice without Parliament
- **1689**: **Bill of Rights**
  - confirmed the rights of Parliament
  - limited the king's power
  - Parliament controlled who could be monarch
  - the monarch had to be **Protestant**
  - a new Parliament had to be elected at least every **3 years**
    - later **7 years**
    - now **5 years**
  - every year the monarch had to ask Parliament to renew funding for the
    army and navy

### Party politics / free press / limits of democracy
- the monarch now needed advisers / ministers to secure support in Parliament
- two main groups in Parliament:
  - **Whigs**
  - **Tories**
- this was the beginning of **party politics**
- **1695**: newspapers no longer needed a government licence
  - helped create a **free press**
- this was the beginning of **constitutional monarchy**
  - monarch still important
  - but could no longer insist on policies if Parliament disagreed
- this was still **not a modern democracy**
  - only men with property of a certain value could vote
  - women could not vote
  - some constituencies were **pocket boroughs**
    - controlled by one wealthy family
  - others were **rotten boroughs**
    - had hardly any voters

## A growing population
- many people left Britain and Ireland for colonies in America and elsewhere
- others came to Britain
- **1656**: first Jews since the Middle Ages settled in **London**
- **1680–1720**: many **Huguenots** came from France
  - Protestants persecuted for their religion
  - many were educated and skilled
  - worked in science, banking, weaving and other crafts

## Act of Union / Great Britain
- **Queen Anne** had no surviving children
- this created uncertainty over succession in England, Wales, Ireland and Scotland
- **1707**: **Act of Union** / **Treaty of Union** in Scotland
  - created the **Kingdom of Great Britain**
- Scotland was no longer independent
- but it kept:
  - its own **legal system**
  - its own **education system**
  - its **Presbyterian Church**

## The Prime Minister
- **1714**: after Queen Anne died, Parliament chose **George I**
  - a German
  - Anne's nearest Protestant relative
- Scottish Jacobites tried to put **James II's son** on the throne
  - the attempt failed
- George I did not speak English well and relied heavily on ministers
- the most important minister became known as the **Prime Minister**
- the first man called Prime Minister was **Sir Robert Walpole**
  - PM from **1721 to 1742**

## Rebellion of the clans
- **1745**: another attempt to restore the Stuarts
- **Charles Edward Stuart** / **Bonnie Prince Charlie**
  - grandson of James II
  - landed in Scotland
  - was supported by Highland clansmen
- at first he had some success
- **1746**: defeated at the **Battle of Culloden**
- he escaped back to Europe

### After Culloden
- clan chiefs lost power
- chieftains became landlords if they had the favour of the English king
- clansmen became tenants paying for land
- this led to the **Highland Clearances**
  - small farms (**crofts**) destroyed
  - land turned into pasture for sheep and cattle
  - evictions became common in the early **19th century**
- many Scots left for **North America**

### Robert Burns
- **Robert Burns** (**1759–96**)
  - known as **The Bard**
  - Scottish poet
  - wrote in Scots, English with Scottish words, and standard English
  - revised many traditional folk songs
- best known work: **Auld Lang Syne**
  - sung at New Year / **Hogmanay**

## The Enlightenment
- the **18th century** saw new ideas in politics, philosophy and science
- this period is called the **Enlightenment**
- many leading thinkers were Scottish
  - **Adam Smith** -> economics
  - **David Hume** -> human nature and philosophy
  - **James Watt** -> steam power / science
- a key Enlightenment principle:
  - everyone should have the right to their own political and religious beliefs
  - the state should not dictate them

## The Industrial Revolution

### Before industrialisation
- before the **18th century**, agriculture was the biggest source of employment
- many people worked in **cottage industries**
  - producing goods like cloth and lace from home

### Industrial Revolution itself
- rapid development of industry in Britain in the **18th and 19th centuries**
- Britain was the first country to industrialise on a large scale
- caused by:
  - development of machinery
  - use of **steam power**
- agriculture and manufacturing became mechanised
- production became faster and more efficient
- coal and raw materials powered factories
- many people moved from countryside to towns
- they worked in:
  - mining
  - manufacturing

### Steel / factories / transport
- **Bessemer process** allowed mass production of steel
- helped develop:
  - shipbuilding
  - railways
- manufacturing became the main source of employment
- canals were built to move raw materials and manufactured goods
- transport links improved, especially in the Midlands and north of England

### Richard Arkwright
- **Richard Arkwright** (**1732–92**)
  - originally a barber and wig-maker
  - moved into textiles
  - improved the **carding machine**
  - developed horse-driven spinning mills using one machine
  - later used steam engines to power machinery
- remembered for running highly efficient and profitable factories

### Working conditions / expansion overseas
- factory working conditions were very poor
- no legal protection for workers
- long hours and dangerous conditions were common
- children worked too, often even more harshly treated
- this was also a period of overseas colonisation
  - **Captain James Cook** mapped Australia
  - Britain gained control over **Canada**
  - the **East India Company** gained control of large parts of **India**
  - colonies were founded in southern **Africa**
- Britain traded worldwide
  - sugar and tobacco from North America and the West Indies
  - textiles, tea and spices from India and Indonesia
- this trade often brought Britain into conflict with **France**

### Sake Dean Mahomet
- **Sake Dean Mahomet** (**1759–1851**)
  - born in Bengal, India
  - came to Britain in **1782**
  - later returned to England after time in Ireland
- **1810**: opened the **Hindoostane Coffee House** in London
  - first curry house in Britain
- also helped introduce **shampooing** (Indian head massage) to Britain

## The slave trade
- British commercial success partly depended on the slave trade
- slavery was illegal inside Britain itself
- but by the **18th century** Britain and the American colonies dominated the
  overseas slave trade
- enslaved people were taken mainly from **West Africa**
- transported in terrible conditions to America and the Caribbean
- forced to work on tobacco and sugar plantations
- living and working conditions were brutal

### Abolition
- many in Britain opposed the slave trade
- **Quakers** formed early anti-slavery groups in the late **1700s**
- **William Wilberforce** played a major role in Parliament
- **1807**: slave trading on British ships / from British ports became illegal
- **1833**: **Emancipation Act**
  - abolished slavery throughout the **British Empire**
- the **Royal Navy** then stopped slave ships from other countries
- after 1833, about **2 million Indian and Chinese workers** replaced freed
  slaves in plantations, mines, railways and the army

## American War of Independence
- by the **1760s**, Britain had large colonies in North America
- colonies were wealthy and mostly controlled their own affairs
- many colonists had originally gone there for **religious freedom**
- they were educated and influenced by ideas of liberty
- the British government wanted to tax them
- colonists argued:
  - **"no taxation without representation"**
- conflict worsened despite attempted compromises
- **1776**: **13 colonies** declared independence
- Britain recognised their independence in **1783**

## War with France
- Britain fought several wars with France in the **18th century**
- **1789**: French Revolution
- later **Napoleon** continued the war against Britain
- **1805**: **Battle of Trafalgar**
  - British navy defeated French and Spanish fleets
  - commanded by **Admiral Nelson**
  - Nelson was killed in battle
- **Nelson's Column** in Trafalgar Square commemorates him
- his ship **HMS Victory** can be visited in Portsmouth
- **1815**: **Battle of Waterloo**
  - **Duke of Wellington** defeated Napoleon
  - Wellington later became Prime Minister
  - known as the **Iron Duke**

## The Union Flag
- Ireland had shared the same monarch as England and Wales since **Henry VIII**
- but it had remained a separate country
- **1801**: after the **Act of Union 1800**, Ireland was unified with England,
  Scotland and Wales
- this created the **United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland**
- the **Union Flag** / **Union Jack** symbolised this union

### Crosses on the Union Flag
- **St George** -> England -> red cross on white
- **St Andrew** -> Scotland -> diagonal white cross on blue
- **St Patrick** -> Ireland -> diagonal red cross on white
- the Welsh dragon is not included
  - because Wales was already united with England when the first Union Flag
    was created in **1606**

## The Victorian Age
- **1837**: **Queen Victoria** became queen aged **18**
- reigned until **1901**
- ruled for almost **64 years**
- her reign is called the **Victorian Age**
- Britain became more powerful and influential abroad
- within Britain:
  - middle classes became more important
  - reformers tried to improve life for the poor

## The British Empire
- during the Victorian period, the British Empire expanded across:
  - all of **India**
  - **Australia**
  - large parts of **Africa**
- it became the largest empire in history
- estimated population: more than **400 million**

### Migration / diversity
- **1853–1913**: about **13 million** Britons left the UK to settle overseas
- people also came into Britain
- **1870–1914**: about **120,000 Russian and Polish Jews** came to escape
  persecution
- many settled in:
  - London's **East End**
  - **Manchester**
  - **Leeds**
- people from the Empire, including **India** and **Africa**, also came to
  Britain to live, work and study

## Trade and industry
- Britain remained a major trading nation
- government promoted **free trade**
- **1846**: **Corn Laws** repealed
  - cheap grain could now be imported
  - raw materials became cheaper
  - this helped British industry grow
- working conditions improved gradually
- **1847**: women and children limited to **10 hours** work per day
- better housing was built for workers

### Railways / Brunel / Great Exhibition
- **George and Robert Stephenson** pioneered the railway engine
- railways expanded greatly during the Victorian period
- railways were built across the Empire
- other engineering advances included bridges and ships

### Isambard Kingdom Brunel
- **Isambard Kingdom Brunel** (**1806–59**)
  - engineer from Portsmouth
  - built tunnels, bridges, railways and ships
  - responsible for the **Great Western Railway**
- the railway ran from **Paddington** to south west England, the West Midlands
  and Wales
- many of his bridges are still used today

### British industrial strength
- in the **19th century**, Britain led the world in industry
- produced more than half the world's:
  - iron
  - coal
  - cotton cloth
- Britain also became a centre for:
  - banking
  - insurance
  - financial services
- **1851**: **Great Exhibition** in **Hyde Park**
  - held in the **Crystal Palace**
  - huge glass-and-steel building
  - showed British and world goods
  - most exhibits were British-made

## The Crimean War
- **1853–1856**: Britain fought with **Turkey** and **France** against **Russia**
- first war to be widely covered by the media with news and photographs
- conditions were terrible
- many soldiers died from disease in hospitals rather than battle wounds
- Queen Victoria introduced the **Victoria Cross**
  - medal for acts of valour

### Florence Nightingale
- **Florence Nightingale** (**1820–1910**)
  - born in Italy to English parents
  - trained as a nurse in Germany
- **1854**: went to Turkey and treated soldiers in military hospitals
- she improved hygiene and reduced death rates
- **1860**: founded the **Nightingale Training School** at **St Thomas' Hospital**
- regarded as the founder of **modern nursing**

## Ireland in the 19th century
- conditions in Ireland were worse than in much of the rest of the UK
- about **two-thirds** of people depended on farming
- many relied heavily on **potatoes**
- when the potato crop failed, Ireland suffered a **famine**
- about **1 million** died
- about **1.5 million** left Ireland
- some went to the **USA**
- others came to **England**
- by **1861**, large Irish communities existed in:
  - Liverpool
  - London
  - Manchester
  - Glasgow

### Irish nationalism
- Irish nationalism grew strongly in the **19th century**
- some, such as the **Fenians**, wanted complete independence
- others, such as **Charles Stuart Parnell**, supported **Home Rule**
  - Ireland would stay in the UK but have its own parliament

## The right to vote

### Reform Acts / Chartists
- as middle classes in industrial towns grew, they demanded more political power
- **1832**: **Reform Act**
  - increased the number of voters
  - abolished pocket and rotten boroughs
  - gave more seats to towns and cities
- political power shifted from countryside to towns
- but voting still depended on **property ownership**
- working-class men still mostly could not vote
- **Chartists** campaigned for wider voting rights
- **1867**: another **Reform Act**
  - created more urban seats
  - reduced property qualifications
- still:
  - most men could not vote
  - women could not vote
- political parties started building organisations to reach ordinary voters
- full **universal suffrage** came in the next century

### Women's rights / suffragettes
- women in 19th-century Britain had fewer rights than men
- until **1870**, a married woman's earnings and property legally belonged to
  her husband
- Acts of Parliament in **1870** and **1882** let wives keep earnings and property
- late 19th / early 20th century:
  - women campaigned for greater rights, especially the vote
  - they became known as **suffragettes**

### Emmeline Pankhurst
- **Emmeline Pankhurst** (**1858–1928**)
  - born in Manchester
- **1889**: founded the **Women's Franchise League**
- **1903**: helped found the **Women's Social and Political Union** (**WSPU**)
  - first group whose members were called **suffragettes**
- tactics included:
  - chaining themselves to railings
  - smashing windows
  - arson
  - hunger strikes
- **1918**:
  - women over **30** got the vote
  - women could also stand for Parliament
- **1928**:
  - women got the vote at **21**, the same as men

## Future of the Empire
- the Empire kept growing until the **1920s**
- but by the late **19th century** people were already debating its future
- supporters said the Empire brought trade and wealth
- critics said it was over-expanded and expensive to defend
- most British people still believed the Empire was a force for good

### Boer War / transition to Commonwealth
- **1899–1902**: **Boer War** in **South Africa**
  - fought against Dutch settlers called the **Boers**
- war lasted over **3 years**
- many died in fighting and disease
- public sympathy for the Boers made some question the Empire
- over time, parts of the Empire gained more autonomy
- eventually there was a transition from **Empire** to **Commonwealth**

### Rudyard Kipling
- **Rudyard Kipling** (**1865–1936**)
  - born in India
  - later lived in India, the UK and the USA
  - wrote books and poems set in India and the UK
- his writing often presented the British Empire as a force for good
- **1907**: won the **Nobel Prize in Literature**
- famous works:
  - **Just So Stories**
  - **The Jungle Book**
  - **If**
