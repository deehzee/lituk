# UK Government, the Law and Your Role — British Constitution

## What a constitution is
- a **constitution** is the set of principles by which a country is governed
- it includes:
  - the institutions that run the country
  - how their power is limited or checked
  - laws and conventions
- the British constitution is described as **"unwritten"**
  - it is not contained in one single document
- this is mainly because the UK has not had a revolution that permanently created
  a completely new system of government
- instead, UK institutions developed gradually over **hundreds of years**
- some people support having one written document
- others think an unwritten constitution allows more **flexibility**

## Constitutional institutions
- the main parts of government are:
  - the **monarchy**
  - **Parliament**
    - **House of Commons**
    - **House of Lords**
  - the **Prime Minister**
  - the **cabinet**
  - the **judiciary** / courts
  - the **police**
  - the **civil service**
  - **local government**
- there are also **devolved governments** in:
  - **Scotland**
  - **Wales**
  - **Northern Ireland**
- these can legislate on certain issues

## The monarchy
- the **monarch** is the **head of state**
- the UK has a **constitutional monarchy**
  - the monarch does **not rule** the country directly
  - the monarch appoints the government chosen through democratic elections
- the monarch invites:
  - the leader of the party with the most MPs
  - or the leader of a coalition
  to become **Prime Minister**
- the monarch meets the Prime Minister regularly
- the monarch can:
  - **advise**
  - **warn**
  - **encourage**
- but government policy is decided by the **Prime Minister and cabinet**

### Ceremonial and symbolic role
- the monarch has important **ceremonial** duties
- one example is opening the new **parliamentary session** each year
- on that occasion, the monarch gives a speech summarising the government's plans
- all **Acts of Parliament** are made in the Majesty's name
- the monarch also represents the UK internationally
  - receives ambassadors and high commissioners
  - entertains visiting heads of state
  - makes state visits abroad
- the monarch provides **stability and continuity**
  - governments and Prime Ministers change
  - the monarch remains head of state
- the monarchy is also a focus for **national identity and pride**

## The national anthem
- the UK national anthem is **God Save the King** / **God Save the Queen**
- it is played at:
  - important national occasions
  - events attended by the monarch or Royal Family
- new citizens swear or affirm loyalty to the monarch at the citizenship ceremony

### Oath / affirmation of allegiance
- new citizens promise loyalty to:
  - **His Majesty King Charles the Third**
  - his **Heirs and Successors**
  - according to law

## System of government
- the UK is a **parliamentary democracy**
- the country is divided into **parliamentary constituencies**
- voters in each constituency elect an **MP**
- all elected MPs together form the **House of Commons**
- most MPs belong to a **political party**
- the party with the most MPs usually forms the government
- if no party has a majority, parties may form a **coalition**

## The House of Commons
- the **House of Commons** is the more important chamber of Parliament
- its members are **democratically elected**
- the **Prime Minister** and almost all cabinet members sit in the Commons
- each MP represents a **constituency**

### Main roles of MPs
- **represent** everyone in their constituency
- help create **new laws**
- **scrutinise** and comment on government actions
- **debate** important national issues

## The House of Lords
- members of the House of Lords are called **peers**
- they are **not elected**
- they do **not** represent constituencies

### Membership
- before **1958**, peers were:
  - **hereditary peers**
  - senior judges
  - bishops of the Church of England
- since **1958**, the Prime Minister can nominate **life peers**
  - appointed only for their own lifetime
  - often people with important careers in politics, business, law or other fields
- life peers are appointed by the monarch on the advice of the Prime Minister
- they can also include people nominated by:
  - leaders of other main political parties
  - an independent **Appointments Commission**
- since **1999**, hereditary peers no longer have an automatic right to sit
- instead, a small number of hereditary peers are elected by their own group to
  represent them

### Role of the House of Lords
- the House of Lords is usually more **independent** of government than the Commons
- it can:
  - suggest **amendments**
  - propose **new laws**
  - check laws passed by the Commons
  - hold the government to account
- many peers are experts in specialist areas
- the Commons can overrule the Lords, but this is not often used

## The Speaker
- debates in the House of Commons are chaired by the **Speaker**
- the Speaker is the chief officer of the Commons
- the Speaker is **neutral**
- even though the Speaker is an MP and has a constituency, they do not act as a
  party politician in the role
- the Speaker is chosen by MPs in a **secret ballot**

### Role of the Speaker
- keeps **order** in debates
- makes sure the rules are followed
- makes sure the **opposition** gets time to debate issues it chooses
- represents Parliament on **ceremonial occasions**

## Elections

### General elections / by-elections
- MPs are elected at a **General Election**
- General Elections are held at least every **5 years**
- if an MP dies or resigns, there is a **by-election**

### First past the post
- MPs are elected by **first past the post**
- in each constituency, the candidate with the **most votes** wins
- the government is usually formed by the party that wins the most constituencies
- if no party wins a majority, parties may form a **coalition**

### European Parliament elections
- the source text also describes elections to the **European Parliament**
- it says these were held every **5 years**
- elected members were called **MEPs**
- it says those elections used **proportional representation**
  - seats allocated according to each party's share of the vote

## Contacting elected representatives
- elected representatives have a duty to serve their constituents
- people can contact MPs by:
  - letter
  - telephone
  - visiting constituency offices
  - attending local **surgeries**
- surgeries are meetings where constituents can raise local concerns in person
