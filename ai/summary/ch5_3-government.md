# UK Government, the Law and Your Role — The Government

## The Prime Minister
- the **Prime Minister (PM)** is the leader of the political party in power
- the PM:
  - appoints the **cabinet**
  - controls many important public appointments
- the PM's official home is:
  - **10 Downing Street**, London
- the PM also has an official country house:
  - **Chequers**

### Changing the Prime Minister
- the PM can be replaced if:
  - MPs in the governing party decide to change the leader
  - the PM chooses to resign
- the PM usually resigns if the party loses a **General Election**

## The cabinet
- the PM appoints about **20 senior MPs** as ministers in charge of departments
- important ministers include:
  - **Chancellor of the Exchequer** -> economy
  - **Home Secretary** -> crime, policing, immigration
  - **Foreign Secretary** -> relations with foreign countries
  - other **Secretaries of State** -> areas such as education, health, defence
- these ministers form the **cabinet**
- the cabinet usually meets **weekly**
- it makes important decisions about **government policy**
- many cabinet decisions must later be:
  - debated in Parliament
  - or approved by Parliament

### Other ministers
- each department also has other ministers:
  - **Ministers of State**
  - **Parliamentary Under-Secretaries of State**
- they are responsible for more specific parts of departmental work

## The opposition
- the **second-largest party** in the House of Commons is called the
  **opposition**
- the leader of the opposition will usually become PM if that party wins the
  next General Election
- the opposition's role is to point out what it sees as the government's:
  - failures
  - weaknesses

### Prime Minister's Questions / shadow cabinet
- an important opportunity for opposition challenge is
  **Prime Minister's Questions**
- this takes place **every week** while Parliament is sitting
- the opposition leader appoints senior MPs as **shadow ministers**
- together they form the **shadow cabinet**
- their role is to:
  - challenge the government
  - offer alternative policies

## The party system
- anyone aged **18 or over** can stand for election as an MP
- in practice, candidates are unlikely to win unless backed by a major party
- major parties mentioned in the text:
  - **Conservative Party**
  - **Labour Party**
  - **Liberal Democrats**
  - parties representing **Scottish, Welsh or Northern Irish** interests
- some MPs are **independents**
  - they do not belong to the main parties
  - they usually focus on a local issue important to their constituency

### Political parties / pressure groups
- political parties look for members of the public to:
  - join debates
  - help fund the party
  - help at elections
- they have branches in most constituencies
- they hold annual **policy-making conferences**
- **pressure groups** and **lobby groups** try to influence government policy
- examples:
  - **CBI** -> business interests
  - **Greenpeace** -> environment
  - **Liberty** -> human rights

## The civil service
- **civil servants** help the government:
  - develop policy
  - implement policy
  - deliver public services
- civil servants are:
  - accountable to ministers
  - chosen on **merit**
  - **politically neutral**
- they are expected to follow core values:
  - **integrity**
  - **honesty**
  - **objectivity**
  - **impartiality**

## Local government
- towns, cities and rural areas are governed by elected councils
  - often called **local authorities**
- some places have:
  - **district councils**
  - **county councils**
- most large towns and cities have one single local authority
- local authorities provide many services
- they are funded by:
  - central government money
  - local taxes

### Mayors / London
- many local authorities appoint a **mayor**
  - usually ceremonial leader
- in some places, the mayor is the effective political leader
- **London** has:
  - **33 local authorities**
  - the **Greater London Authority**
  - the **Mayor of London**
- local elections for councillors are usually held in **May**

## Devolved administrations
- since **1997**, some powers have been devolved from the UK government to:
  - **Wales**
  - **Scotland**
  - **Northern Ireland**
- this gives people in those parts of the UK more control over local matters
- there has been:
  - a **Welsh Assembly** since **1999**
  - a **Scottish Parliament** since **1999**
  - a **Northern Ireland Assembly**, though sometimes suspended

### Reserved vs devolved matters
- some important areas remain under central UK government control:
  - **defence**
  - **foreign affairs**
  - **immigration**
  - **taxation**
  - **social security**
- many public services are devolved, such as:
  - **education**
- each devolved administration has its own **civil service**

## The Welsh government
- based in **Cardiff**
- the **National Assembly for Wales** has **60 Assembly Members (AMs)**
- elections are held every **4 years**
- uses a form of **proportional representation**
- members can speak in:
  - **Welsh**
  - **English**
- all publications are available in both languages

### Powers of the Welsh Assembly
- can make laws in **20 areas**, including:
  - education and training
  - health and social services
  - economic development
  - housing
- since **2011**, it has been able to pass laws in these areas without UK
  Parliament's agreement

## The Scottish Parliament
- created in **1999**
- sits in **Edinburgh**
- has **129 MSPs** (**Members of the Scottish Parliament**)
- elected by a form of **proportional representation**
- can make laws for Scotland on matters not reserved to the UK Parliament

### Areas it can legislate on
- **civil and criminal law**
- **health**
- **education**
- **planning**
- additional **tax-raising powers**

## The Northern Ireland Assembly
- there was a Northern Ireland Parliament from **1922**
- it was abolished in **1972**, shortly after the Troubles began
- the **Northern Ireland Assembly** was created after the
  **Belfast / Good Friday Agreement** in **1998**
- it works with a **power-sharing** arrangement between the main parties
- it has **90 MLAs** (**Members of the Legislative Assembly**)
- they are elected by a form of **proportional representation**

### Areas it can decide on
- **education**
- **agriculture**
- **environment**
- **health**
- **social services**

### Suspension
- the UK government can suspend devolved assemblies
- it has done this several times in Northern Ireland
- the Assembly has operated successfully since **2007**

## The media and government
- Parliament is broadcast on television
- proceedings are published in official reports called **Hansard**
- many people learn about politics from:
  - newspapers / the press
  - television
  - radio
  - the internet

### Free press / broadcasting rules
- the UK has a **free press**
  - newspapers are free from government control
- some newspaper owners and editors campaign to influence opinion and policy
- by law, radio and TV political coverage must be **balanced**
- rival viewpoints must be given equal time

## Who can vote?
- the UK has had a fully democratic voting system since **1928**
- the voting age has been **18** since **1969**
- with a few exceptions, all UK-born and naturalised adult citizens can vote

### Voting rights
- adult citizens of:
  - the **UK**
  - the **Commonwealth**
  - the **Irish Republic**
  who live in the UK can vote in all public elections
- adult citizens of other **EU states** resident in the UK can vote in all
  elections except **General Elections**

## The electoral register
- to vote in parliamentary, local or European elections, your name must be on
  the **electoral register**
- eligible people can register through their **local council electoral
  registration office**
- the electoral register is updated every year in **September or October**
- forms are sent to each household and must list everyone eligible to vote there

### Northern Ireland registration
- Northern Ireland uses **individual registration**
- each eligible person fills in their own form
- once registered, a person stays on the register unless details change

### Public access
- by law, local authorities must make the register available for inspection
- it can be seen at:
  - electoral registration offices
  - council offices
  - some public buildings such as libraries

## Where to vote
- people vote at **polling stations**
  - in Scotland also called **polling places**
- before the election, voters are sent a **poll card**
  - says where to vote
  - says when the election will take place
- polling stations are open from **7.00 am to 10.00 pm**

### Voting process
- at the polling station, staff ask for your:
  - name
  - address
- in **Northern Ireland**, photographic ID is also required
- you are then given a **ballot paper**
- you fill it in privately in a **polling booth**
- you then place it in the **ballot box**
- nobody has the right to force you to vote for a particular candidate

### Postal voting
- if you cannot get to a polling station, you can register for a
  **postal ballot**
- the ballot paper is sent to your home
- you fill it in and post it back

## Standing for office
- most citizens of:
  - the **UK**
  - the **Irish Republic**
  - the **Commonwealth**
  aged **18 or over** can stand for public office
- exceptions include:
  - members of the armed forces
  - civil servants
  - people convicted of certain criminal offences
- members of the **House of Lords** cannot stand for the House of Commons
  but can stand for other public offices

## Visiting Parliament and devolved administrations

### UK Parliament
- the public can watch debates in the **Palace of Westminster**
- debates can be watched from public galleries in:
  - the **House of Commons**
  - the **House of Lords**
- entry is free
- people can:
  - write to their local MP for tickets
  - or queue on the day
- it is usually easier to get into the House of Lords than the Commons

### Northern Ireland Assembly
- MLAs meet at **Stormont** in **Belfast**
- visits can be arranged through:
  - the **Education Service**
  - or an **MLA**

### Scottish Parliament
- MSPs meet at **Holyrood** in **Edinburgh**
- visitors can get information, book tickets or arrange tours through visitor services

### National Assembly for Wales
- AMs meet in the **Senedd** in **Cardiff Bay**
- the Senedd is an **open building**
- people can book:
  - guided tours
  - seats in the public galleries
