# A Modern, Thriving Society — Leisure

## Leisure in the UK
- people in the UK spend their free time in many different ways

## Gardening
- many people have a **garden** at home and spend time looking after it
- some rent extra land called an **allotment**
  - used for growing fruit and vegetables
- there are gardening and flower shows from local events to national exhibitions
- many towns have **garden centres**

### Famous gardens
- **Kew Gardens**
- **Sissinghurst**
- **Hidcote**
- **Crathes Castle**
- **Inveraray Castle**
- **Bodnant Garden**
- **Mount Stewart**

### National flowers
- **England**: **rose**
- **Scotland**: **thistle**
- **Wales**: **daffodil**
- **Northern Ireland**: **shamrock**

## Shopping
- most towns and cities have a central shopping area called the **town centre**
- **shopping centres** are also common
  - in town centres
  - or on the outskirts
- most shops open **7 days a week**
- on Sundays and public holidays, hours are usually shorter
- many towns also have regular **markets**

## Cooking and food
- many people in the UK enjoy cooking
- people often invite each other home for dinner
- food in the UK is varied because of its diverse population and cultural heritage

### Traditional foods
- **England**
  - **roast beef** with potatoes, vegetables and **Yorkshire pudding**
  - **fish and chips**
- **Wales**
  - **Welsh cakes**
- **Scotland**
  - **haggis**
- **Northern Ireland**
  - **Ulster fry**

## Films

### British film industry
- the UK has had a major influence on modern cinema
- films were first shown publicly in the UK in **1896**
- British film-makers became known early for **special effects**
- British actors have often worked in both the UK and the USA
- **Charlie Chaplin** became famous for his **tramp** character in silent films

### Directors / studios / strengths
- British studios flourished in the **1930s**
- important directors included:
  - **Sir Alexander Korda**
  - **Sir Alfred Hitchcock**
  - later **Sir David Lean**
  - **Ridley Scott**
- British films in the Second World War helped boost morale
- the **1950s** and **1960s** were a strong period for British comedy films
- many films made in the UK today are produced by foreign companies using British expertise
- Britain remains especially strong in:
  - **special effects**
  - **animation**
- **Ealing Studios** is one of the oldest continuously working film studios
- **Nick Park** won **4 Oscars**
  - including **3** for **Wallace and Gromit**

### Actors / awards
- famous British actors mentioned:
  - **Sir Laurence Olivier**
  - **David Niven**
  - **Sir Rex Harrison**
  - **Richard Burton**
- later British Oscar winners mentioned:
  - **Colin Firth**
  - **Sir Anthony Hopkins**
  - **Dame Judi Dench**
  - **Kate Winslet**
  - **Tilda Swinton**
- the **BAFTA Awards** are the British equivalent of the Oscars

## British comedy
- comedy, satire and laughing at ourselves are an important part of UK culture
- in medieval times, kings and nobles had **jesters**
- later, **Shakespeare** included comic characters in his plays
- in the **18th century**, political cartoons became popular
- in the **19th century**, satirical magazines appeared
  - the most famous was **Punch**
- today, satire continues in newspapers and magazines such as **Private Eye**

### Comedy on stage / TV
- comedians were important in **music hall**
- some music hall performers later became TV stars
  - e.g. **Morecambe and Wise**
- TV comedy developed its own style
- **sitcoms** remain popular
- satire also remained important
  - **That Was The Week That Was**
  - **Spitting Image**
- **Monty Python's Flying Circus** (**1969**) introduced a new style of comedy
- **stand-up comedy** has become popular again in recent years

## Television and radio

### Television
- the UK has many TV channels
  - some free
  - some subscription
- popular programmes include soap operas such as:
  - **Coronation Street**
  - **EastEnders**
- Scotland has Scotland-specific programmes and a Gaelic-language channel
- Wales has a Welsh-language channel
- Northern Ireland has its own regional programming and some Irish Gaelic programmes

### TV licence / BBC
- anyone watching TV on a TV, computer or other device needs a **TV licence**
- one licence covers one home
- people renting separate rooms under separate tenancies each need their own
- people over **75** can apply for a free licence
- blind people can get a **50% discount**
- watching TV without a licence can lead to a fine of up to **£1,000**
- licence money funds the **BBC**
  - the UK's public service broadcaster
  - largest broadcaster in the world
  - wholly state-funded but independent of government
- other UK channels are mainly funded by:
  - advertisements
  - subscriptions

### Radio
- the UK also has many radio stations
  - national
  - local / regional
  - music-specific
  - Welsh / Gaelic language stations
- **BBC radio** is funded by TV licences
- other radio stations are funded by advertising

## Social networking
- sites such as **Facebook** and **Twitter** are popular
- people use them to:
  - stay in touch
  - organise events
  - share photos, videos and opinions
- many use them on mobile phones

## Pubs and night clubs
- **pubs** are an important part of social life in the UK
- many communities have a **local pub**
- popular pub activities include:
  - **pub quizzes**
  - **pool**
  - **darts**
- to buy alcohol in a pub or night club, you must be **18 or over**
- people under 18 may be allowed in some pubs with an adult
- **16-year-olds** can drink wine or beer with a meal in a hotel or restaurant
  if they are with someone over 18
- pubs usually open from **11.00 am**
  - **12 noon on Sundays**
- night clubs usually open later and close later than pubs

## Betting and gambling
- betting on sports and other events is common
- there are also **casinos**
- you must be **18** to enter betting shops or gambling clubs
- the **National Lottery** has weekly draws
- people can enter by buying:
  - a ticket
  - a scratch card
- people under **18** are not allowed to take part in the National Lottery

## Pets
- many people in the UK keep pets such as cats and dogs
- it is against the law to:
  - treat a pet cruelly
  - neglect a pet
- dogs in public must wear a collar showing the owner's name and address
- owners must:
  - keep the dog under control
  - clean up after it in public
- animals can be treated by **vets**
- some charities help people who cannot afford vet bills
