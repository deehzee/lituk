# UK Government, the Law and Your Role — Fundamental Principles

## Fundamental principles of UK law
- Britain has a long history of respecting **individual rights** and protecting
  essential **freedoms**
- these rights have roots in:
  - **Magna Carta**
  - **Habeas Corpus Act**
  - **Bill of Rights (1689)**
- British diplomats and lawyers helped draft the
  **European Convention on Human Rights**
- the UK was one of the first countries to sign it in **1950**

### Rights in the European Convention on Human Rights
- **right to life**
- **prohibition of torture**
- **prohibition of slavery and forced labour**
- **right to liberty and security**
- **right to a fair trial**
- **freedom of thought, conscience and religion**
- **freedom of expression**

### Human Rights Act
- **Human Rights Act 1998**
  - brought the European Convention on Human Rights into UK law
- the following must follow its principles:
  - the **government**
  - **public bodies**
  - the **courts**

## Equal opportunities
- UK law protects people from unfair treatment because of:
  - **age**
  - **disability**
  - **sex**
  - **pregnancy and maternity**
  - **race**
  - **religion or belief**
  - **sexuality**
  - **marital status**
- people facing discrimination can get help from:
  - **Citizens Advice**
  - equality / human rights bodies in each part of the UK

## Domestic violence
- **domestic violence** is a serious crime in the UK
- violence against a partner can lead to prosecution
  - whether the couple is married or living together
  - whether the abuser is a man or a woman
- a man who forces a woman to have sex, including his wife, can be charged
  with **rape**
- people facing domestic violence should seek help as soon as possible
- help can come from:
  - a **solicitor**
  - **Citizens Advice**
  - **refuges** / **shelters**
  - the **police**
  - the **24-hour National Domestic Violence Freephone Helpline**

## Female genital mutilation (FGM)
- **FGM** is illegal in the UK
- it is also illegal to take a girl or woman abroad for FGM
- practising FGM or arranging it is a **criminal offence**

## Forced marriage
- a marriage must be entered into with the **full and free consent** of both people
- **arranged marriage** is legal if both people agree
- **forced marriage** is illegal
- forcing someone to marry is a **criminal offence**

### Protection orders
- **Forced Marriage Protection Orders** were introduced:
  - **2008** in England, Wales and Northern Ireland
  - **2011** in Scotland
- these orders can protect:
  - someone at risk of forced marriage
  - someone already in a forced marriage
- a victim or someone acting for them can apply
- breaking an order can lead to prison for up to **2 years**

## Taxation

### Income tax
- people in the UK pay **income tax** on income such as:
  - wages
  - self-employment profits
  - taxable benefits
  - pensions
  - income from property, savings and dividends
- income tax helps pay for government services such as:
  - roads
  - education
  - police
  - armed forces

### PAYE / self-assessment
- for most employees, tax is taken automatically by the employer
- this system is called **PAYE** (**Pay As You Earn**)
- self-employed people usually pay tax through **self-assessment**
- this includes completing a **tax return**
- if HMRC sends you a tax return, you must complete and return it

## National Insurance
- almost everyone in paid work, including self-employed people, must pay
  **National Insurance Contributions**
- National Insurance helps fund:
  - **state benefits**
  - **state retirement pension**
  - **NHS**
- employees have contributions deducted by their employer
- self-employed people must pay their own contributions
- people who do not pay enough contributions may not qualify for some benefits
  or a full state pension

## National Insurance number
- a **National Insurance number** is a unique personal account number
- it makes sure tax and National Insurance payments are correctly recorded
- young people in the UK receive one before their **16th birthday**
- non-UK nationals looking for work, starting work or becoming self-employed
  usually need one
- you can start work **without** one
- but if you have permission to work, you must apply through the relevant system
- a National Insurance number **does not by itself prove** a right to work

## Driving

### Basic requirements
- you must be at least **17** to drive a car or motor cycle
- you must have a **driving licence**
- to get a UK licence, you must pass:
  - a **theory test**
    - multiple choice
    - hazard perception
  - a **practical driving test**
- you must be at least **16** to ride a **moped**
- there are extra age rules and tests for large vehicles

### Provisional licence / licence length
- the first step is getting a **provisional driving licence**
- drivers can normally use their licence until age **70**
- after that, it is renewed every **3 years**
- in **Northern Ireland**, newly qualified drivers must display an **R plate**
  for **1 year**

### Using foreign licences
- if your licence is from the **EU**, **Iceland**, **Liechtenstein** or
  **Norway**, you can drive in the UK while it remains valid
- licences from other countries can usually be used for up to **12 months**
- after that, you must get a **UK full driving licence**

### Vehicle rules
- if you are resident in the UK, your vehicle must be registered with the
  **DVLA**
- you must:
  - pay **annual road tax**
  - have valid **motor insurance**
- driving without insurance is a serious criminal offence
- if a vehicle is over **3 years old**, it must pass an annual **MOT test**
- not having an MOT certificate for an older vehicle is an offence
