# A Modern, Thriving Society — Customs and Traditions

## Main Christian festivals

### Christmas
- **25 December**: **Christmas Day**
  - celebrates the birth of **Jesus Christ**
  - public holiday
- many Christians go to church on:
  - **Christmas Eve** (**24 December**)
  - or **Christmas Day**
- common Christmas traditions:
  - special meal at home
  - **roast turkey**
  - **Christmas pudding**
  - **mince pies**
  - giving gifts
  - sending cards
  - decorating houses
  - decorating a **Christmas tree**
- very young children often believe **Father Christmas** / **Santa Claus**
  brings presents during the night before Christmas Day

### Boxing Day
- **26 December**: **Boxing Day**
- public holiday
- day after Christmas Day

### Easter
- **Easter** takes place in **March or April**
- it marks:
  - the death of Jesus Christ on **Good Friday**
  - his resurrection on **Easter Sunday**
- **Good Friday** and **Easter Monday** are public holidays
- Easter is also celebrated by many non-religious people
  - **Easter eggs** are chocolate eggs given as presents
  - they symbolise **new life**

### Lent / Shrove Tuesday / Ash Wednesday
- the **40 days before Easter** are called **Lent**
- Lent is a time for reflection and preparation
- traditionally, Christians **fasted**
- many people today give up something they enjoy, such as a favourite food
- the day before Lent is **Shrove Tuesday** / **Pancake Day**
  - people eat pancakes
  - originally to use up eggs, fat and milk before fasting
- Lent begins on **Ash Wednesday**
  - some church services mark people with an **ash cross** on the forehead

## Other religious festivals

### Diwali
- usually in **October or November**
- lasts **5 days**
- called the **Festival of Lights**
- celebrated by **Hindus** and **Sikhs**
- celebrates:
  - victory of good over evil
  - gaining of knowledge
- there is a famous Diwali celebration in **Leicester**

### Hannukah
- in **November or December**
- lasts **8 days**
- remembers the Jewish struggle for religious freedom
- one candle is lit each day on a **menorah**
- this recalls the story of oil lasting **8 days** instead of 1

### Eid al-Fitr
- celebrates the end of **Ramadan**
- Muslims thank **Allah** for the strength to complete the fast
- the date changes every year
- Muslims mark it with:
  - special services
  - special meals

### Eid ul Adha
- remembers that the prophet **Ibrahim** was willing to sacrifice his son
  when God commanded him
- reminds Muslims of commitment to God
- many Muslims sacrifice an animal to eat during the festival
- in Britain, this must be done in a **slaughterhouse**

### Vaisakhi / Baisakhi
- Sikh festival
- celebrates the founding of the **Khalsa**
- held on **14 April** each year
- celebrated with:
  - parades
  - dancing
  - singing

## Other festivals and traditions

### New Year / Hogmanay
- **1 January**: **New Year**
  - public holiday
- people usually celebrate on the night of **31 December**
  - **New Year's Eve**
- in **Scotland**, 31 December is called **Hogmanay**
- **2 January** is also a public holiday in Scotland
- for some Scots, Hogmanay is a bigger celebration than Christmas

### Valentine's Day
- **14 February**
- lovers exchange cards and gifts
- sometimes cards are sent anonymously

### April Fool's Day
- **1 April**
- day for jokes and tricks
- TV and newspapers often publish fake April Fool stories
- jokes are played until **midday**

### Mothering Sunday / Father's Day
- **Mothering Sunday** / **Mother's Day**
  - Sunday **3 weeks before Easter**
  - children give cards or gifts to their mothers
- **Father's Day**
  - **third Sunday in June**
  - children give cards or gifts to their fathers

### Halloween
- **31 October**
- ancient festival with roots in a pagan festival marking the start of winter
- common traditions:
  - dressing in frightening costumes
  - **trick or treat**
  - carving lanterns from **pumpkins**
  - putting candles inside them

### Bonfire Night
- **5 November**
- people in **Great Britain** celebrate with fireworks
- remembers the failed plot of **1605**
- **Guy Fawkes** and other Catholics tried to kill the Protestant king
  with a bomb in the **Houses of Parliament**

### Remembrance Day
- **11 November**
- commemorates those who died fighting for the UK and its allies
- originally marked the end of the **First World War** on **11 November 1918**
- people wear **poppies**
- at **11.00 am** there is a **2-minute silence**
- wreaths are laid at the **Cenotaph** in **Whitehall, London**

## Bank holidays
- there are also public holidays called **bank holidays**
- on these days:
  - banks close
  - many businesses close
- they have **no religious significance**
- they take place:
  - at the **beginning of May**
  - in **late May or early June**
  - in **August**
- in **Northern Ireland**, the anniversary of the **Battle of the Boyne**
  in **July** is also a public holiday
