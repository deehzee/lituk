# Middle Ages

## War at home and abroad
- period after the **Norman Conquest** until about **1485**
- also called the **Middle Ages** / **medieval period**
- time of almost constant war

### Wales
- English kings fought Welsh noblemen for control
- **1284**: **King Edward I** introduced the **Statute of Rhuddlan**
  - annexed **Wales** to the **Crown of England**
- huge castles built to maintain English power
  - **Conwy**
  - **Caernarvon**
- by the middle of the **15th century**, the last Welsh rebellions were defeated
- English laws and the English language were introduced

### Scotland
- English kings were less successful in Scotland
- **1314**: **Robert the Bruce** led the Scots to victory over the English at the
  **Battle of Bannockburn**
- Scotland remained unconquered by the English

### Ireland
- at the beginning of the Middle Ages, **Ireland** was independent
- the English first went there as troops to help the Irish king
- they then stayed and built their own settlements
- by **1200**, the English ruled an area around Dublin called **the Pale**
- some lords in other parts of Ireland accepted the authority of the English king

### Wars abroad
- many knights took part in the **Crusades**
  - European Christians fought for control of the **Holy Land**
- English kings also fought France in the **Hundred Years War**
  - actually lasted **116 years**
- **1415**: **Battle of Agincourt**
  - **King Henry V**'s vastly outnumbered English army defeated the French
- the English left France in the **1450s**

## The Black Death

### Feudalism / land ownership
- Normans used a system of land ownership called **feudalism**
- the king gave land to lords in return for help in war
- landowners had to send men to serve in the army
- some peasants had their own land, but most were **serfs**
- serfs:
  - had a small area of their lord's land to grow food
  - had to work for their lord
  - could not move away
- the same system developed in southern Scotland
- in the north of Scotland and in Ireland, land was owned by **clans**
  - prominent families

### The Black Death itself
- **1348**: a disease, probably a form of plague, came to Britain
- this was called the **Black Death**
- about **one third** of the population of England died
- a similar proportion died in **Scotland** and **Wales**
- one of the worst disasters ever to strike Britain

### Effects of the Black Death
- smaller population -> less need to grow cereal crops
- labour shortages developed
- peasants began to demand higher wages
- new social classes appeared
  - including large landowners later called the **gentry**
- people left the countryside and moved to towns
- growing wealth in towns helped create a strong **middle class**
- in Ireland, many in **the Pale** died and for a time the English-controlled area became smaller

## Legal and political changes

### Parliament
- Parliament began to develop into the institution it is today
- it grew out of the king's council of advisers
  - important noblemen
  - leaders of the Church

### Magna Carta
- before **1215**, there were few formal limits on the king's power
- **1215**: **King John** was forced by his noblemen to accept demands
- result: **Magna Carta** (**Great Charter**)
- Magna Carta established that even the king was subject to the law
- it:
  - protected the rights of the nobility
  - restricted the king's power to collect taxes
  - restricted the king's power to make or change laws
- from then on, the king had to involve his noblemen in decisions

### Houses of Parliament
- in England, parliaments were called mainly so the king could consult nobles,
  especially when he needed money
- Parliament later split into **two Houses**:
  - **House of Lords**
    - nobility
    - great landowners
    - bishops
  - **House of Commons**
    - knights (usually smaller landowners)
    - wealthy people from towns and cities
- only a small part of the population could help elect members of the Commons

### Scotland and law
- Scotland developed a similar Parliament
  - had **three Houses / Estates**:
    - lords
    - commons
    - clergy
- judges began to become independent of government
- in England, judges developed **common law**
  - based on **precedence** and tradition
- in Scotland, laws were more **codified**
  - written down

## A distinct identity

### Language
- after the Norman Conquest:
  - king and nobles spoke **Norman French**
  - peasants continued to speak **Anglo-Saxon**
- the two gradually merged into one **English language**
- examples:
  - Norman French: **park**, **beauty**, **demand**
  - Anglo-Saxon: **apple**, **cow**, **summer**, **ask**
- by **1400**:
  - official documents in England were being written in English
  - English had become the language of the royal court and Parliament

### Literature
- before **1400**, **Geoffrey Chaucer** wrote **The Canterbury Tales**
  - poems about pilgrims travelling to Canterbury and telling stories
- it became one of the first books printed by **William Caxton**
  - first person in England to print books using a printing press
- many of the stories are still popular

### Scotland / culture
- in Scotland, many people still spoke **Gaelic**
- the **Scots** language also developed
- poets wrote in Scots
  - e.g. **John Barbour** wrote **The Bruce** about Bannockburn

### Buildings / trade
- many castles were built in Britain and Ireland for defence
  - some are now ruins
  - some, like **Windsor** and **Edinburgh**, are still in use
- great cathedrals were also built
  - e.g. **Lincoln Cathedral**
- many had stained-glass windows telling Bible and saints' stories
  - famous example: **York Minster**
- England became an important trading nation
- **English wool** became an important export
- skilled workers came from abroad
  - weavers from **France**
  - engineers from **Germany**
  - glass manufacturers from **Italy**
  - canal builders from **Holland**

## Wars of the Roses
- **1455**: civil war began over who should be king of England
- fought between two families:
  - **House of Lancaster**
    - symbol: **red rose**
  - **House of York**
    - symbol: **white rose**
- this conflict was called the **Wars of the Roses**
- ended at the **Battle of Bosworth Field** in **1485**
- **King Richard III** of the House of York was killed
- **Henry Tudor**, leader of the House of Lancaster, became **King Henry VII**
- Henry married **Elizabeth of York**
  - united the two families
- Henry VII became the first king of the **House of Tudor**
- symbol of the House of Tudor:
  - a **red rose with a white rose inside it**
  - showed York and Lancaster were now allies
