# Tudors and Stuarts

## Religious conflicts

### Henry VII / Henry VIII
- after winning the **Wars of the Roses**, **Henry VII** wanted England to stay
  peaceful and his throne secure
- he:
  - strengthened the central administration
  - reduced the power of the nobles
  - built up the monarchy's financial reserves
- his son **Henry VIII** continued this policy of centralising power
- Henry VIII is most famous for:
  - breaking away from the **Church of Rome**
  - marrying **six times**

### The six wives of Henry VIII
- **Catherine of Aragon**
  - Spanish princess
  - only surviving child with Henry was **Mary**
  - Henry wanted to divorce her to try for a male heir
- **Anne Boleyn**
  - English
  - mother of **Elizabeth**
  - accused of taking lovers and executed at the **Tower of London**
- **Jane Seymour**
  - gave Henry his son **Edward**
  - died soon after the birth
- **Anne of Cleves**
  - German princess
  - political marriage
  - divorced soon after
- **Catherine Howard**
  - cousin of Anne Boleyn
  - accused of taking lovers and executed
- **Catherine Parr**
  - widow
  - survived Henry
  - married again but died soon after

### Break with Rome / Reformation
- Henry needed the **Pope's** approval to divorce Catherine of Aragon
- when the Pope refused, Henry established the **Church of England**
- in this new Church:
  - the **king**, not the Pope, appointed bishops
  - the king decided how people should worship
- at the same time, the **Reformation** was spreading across Europe
- Protestants:
  - opposed the authority of the Pope
  - read the Bible in their own languages, not Latin
  - did not pray to saints or at shrines
  - believed a person's own relationship with God was more important than
    submitting to Church authority
- Protestant ideas grew in **England, Wales and Scotland** during the
  **16th century**

### Wales / Ireland / later Tudor monarchs
- in **Ireland**, English attempts to impose Protestantism and English laws of
  inheritance led to rebellion by Irish chieftains and brutal fighting
- during Henry VIII's reign, **Wales** became formally united with England by
  the **Act for the Government of Wales**
  - Welsh representatives were sent to the **House of Commons**
  - the Welsh legal system was reformed
- Henry VIII was succeeded by **Edward VI**
  - strongly Protestant
  - **Book of Common Prayer** written during his reign
- Edward died aged **15** after ruling just over **six years**
- his half-sister **Mary** became queen
  - devout Catholic
  - persecuted Protestants
  - known as **Bloody Mary**
- after Mary's short reign, her half-sister **Elizabeth I** became queen

## Queen Elizabeth I
- **Elizabeth I** was Protestant
- she re-established the **Church of England** as the official Church in England
- everyone had to attend their local church
- laws controlled religious services and prayers
- Elizabeth did not ask about people's real beliefs
- she balanced Catholic views and more extreme Protestant views
- this helped avoid serious religious conflict in England
- she became one of the most popular English monarchs
- **1588**: the English defeated the **Spanish Armada**
  - Spain had sent the fleet to conquer England and restore Catholicism

## Reformation in Scotland / Mary, Queen of Scots

### Scotland
- Scotland was also strongly influenced by Protestant ideas
- **1560**: the Scottish Parliament:
  - abolished the authority of the Pope in Scotland
  - made Roman Catholic services illegal
- a Protestant **Church of Scotland** was established
- unlike in England, it was **not a state Church**

### Mary, Queen of Scots
- **Mary Stuart** / **Mary, Queen of Scots** was Catholic
- she became queen when she was only **one week old**
- much of her childhood was spent in **France**
- after returning to Scotland, she became the centre of a power struggle
- when her husband was murdered, Mary was suspected of involvement
- she fled to England
- she gave her throne to her Protestant son, **James VI of Scotland**
- Mary was **Elizabeth I's cousin**
- Elizabeth kept her prisoner for **20 years**
- Mary was eventually executed for plotting against Elizabeth I

## Exploration, poetry and drama

### Exploration / colonisation
- the Elizabethan period was a time of growing English patriotism
- English explorers looked for new trade routes and tried to expand trade into
  Spanish colonies in the Americas
- **Sir Francis Drake**:
  - one of the commanders in the defeat of the Spanish Armada
  - one of the founders of England's naval tradition
  - his ship, the **Golden Hind**, was one of the first to sail around the world
- in Elizabeth's time, English settlers first began to colonise the eastern
  coast of America
- this colonisation increased in the next century

### Shakespeare
- the Elizabethan period is also remembered for its poetry and drama,
  especially **William Shakespeare**
- **William Shakespeare** (**1564–1616**):
  - born in **Stratford-upon-Avon**
  - playwright and actor
  - wrote poems and plays
- famous plays:
  - **A Midsummer Night's Dream**
  - **Hamlet**
  - **Macbeth**
  - **Romeo and Juliet**
- he also portrayed ordinary Englishmen and women, not just kings and queens
- he had a huge influence on the English language
- famous lines include:
  - **Once more unto the breach**
  - **To be or not to be**
  - **A rose by any other name**
  - **All the world's a stage**
  - **The darling buds of May**
- the **Globe Theatre** in London is a modern copy of the theatres where his
  plays were first performed

## James VI and I / King James Bible
- **Elizabeth I** never married and had no children
- when she died in **1603**, her heir was **James VI of Scotland**
- he became **James I of England, Wales and Ireland**
- Scotland still remained a separate country

### King James Bible
- one major achievement of James's reign was a new English translation of the
  Bible
- called:
  - the **King James Version**
  - the **Authorised Version**
- it was not the first English Bible
- it is still used in many Protestant churches today

## Ireland
- during this period, Ireland was almost completely **Catholic**
- **Henry VII** and **Henry VIII** extended English control outside the Pale
- Henry VIII took the title **King of Ireland**
- English laws were introduced
- local leaders were expected to follow the Lord Lieutenants in **Dublin**
- under **Elizabeth I** and **James I**, many people in Ireland opposed rule by
  the Protestant government in England
- there were several rebellions
- the English government encouraged Scottish and English Protestants to settle
  in **Ulster**
  - these settlements were called **plantations**
  - land was taken from Catholic landholders
- many settlers came from south-west Scotland
- more land was given to companies based in London
- James organised similar plantations in other parts of Ireland
- this had serious long-term consequences for England, Scotland and Ireland

## The rise of Parliament
- **Elizabeth I** was skilled at managing Parliament
- she balanced her own wishes against those of:
  - the **House of Lords**
  - the **House of Commons**
- **James I** and **Charles I** were less skilled
- both believed in the **Divine Right of Kings**
  - the idea that the king was appointed directly by God
- they believed the king should rule without needing Parliament's approval
- **Charles I** tried to rule this way
- when Parliament would not agree with his religious and foreign policies,
  he ruled without Parliament
- for **11 years**, he raised money without Parliament's approval
- eventually trouble in Scotland forced him to recall Parliament

## Beginning of the English Civil War
- Charles I wanted more ceremony in Church of England worship
- he introduced a revised **Prayer Book**
- he tried to impose it on the **Presbyterian Church in Scotland**
- this caused serious unrest
- a Scottish army was formed
- Charles needed money for his own army, so in **1640** he recalled Parliament
- many MPs were **Puritans**
  - Protestants who wanted strict and simple worship
- Parliament refused to give Charles the money he wanted
- a rebellion also began in Ireland
- Parliament demanded control of the English army
- Charles I entered the **House of Commons** and tried to arrest five leaders
  - they had already escaped
  - no monarch has entered the Commons since
- **1642**: civil war began
- the country split into:
  - **Cavaliers** -> supported the king
  - **Roundheads** -> supported Parliament

## Oliver Cromwell and the English Republic
- the king's army was defeated at:
  - **Marston Moor**
  - **Naseby**
- by **1646**, Parliament had won the war
- Charles I was held prisoner
- he still refused agreement with Parliament
- **1649**: Charles I was executed

### Commonwealth / Cromwell
- England became a republic called the **Commonwealth**
- there was no monarch
- the army was in control
- **Oliver Cromwell** went to Ireland to crush revolt and Royalist forces
- he established Parliament's authority there
- his violence in Ireland made him controversial to this day
- the Scots declared **Charles II** king
- Cromwell defeated Charles II's forces at:
  - **Dunbar**
  - **Worcester**
- Charles II escaped, at one point hiding in an **oak tree**
- Parliament now controlled **Scotland** as well as **England and Wales**
- Cromwell became leader of the republic with the title **Lord Protector**
- he ruled until his death in **1658**
- his son **Richard Cromwell** succeeded him but could not control the army or
  government
- Britain had been a republic for **11 years**
- without Cromwell, there was no stable system or clear leader
- many people wanted the monarchy back

## The Restoration
- **1660**: Parliament invited **Charles II** back from exile in the Netherlands
- he became king of **England, Wales, Scotland and Ireland**
- Charles II accepted that he sometimes had to work with Parliament
- the **Church of England** became the established Church again
- both **Roman Catholics** and **Puritans** were kept out of power

### Plague / fire / law / science
- **1665**: major plague outbreak in **London**
- **1666**: **Great Fire of London**
  - destroyed much of the city, including many churches and **St Paul's Cathedral**
- London was rebuilt
- new **St Paul's** was designed by **Sir Christopher Wren**
- **Samuel Pepys** wrote about these events in his diary
- **1679**: **Habeas Corpus Act**
  - means no one can be held prisoner unlawfully
  - every prisoner has a right to a court hearing
- Charles II was interested in science
- during his reign, the **Royal Society** was formed
  - to promote natural knowledge
  - oldest surviving scientific society in the world
- early members included:
  - **Sir Edmund Halley**
  - **Sir Isaac Newton**

### Isaac Newton
- **Isaac Newton** (**1643–1727**):
  - born in **Lincolnshire**
  - studied at **Cambridge University**
  - became one of the most important scientists in history
- his most famous work was **Principia Mathematica**
  - showed how gravity applied to the universe
- he also discovered that white light is made up of the colours of the rainbow

## A Catholic King
- **Charles II** had no legitimate children
- he died in **1685**
- his brother became:
  - **James II** in England, Wales and Ireland
  - **James VII** in Scotland
- James was **Roman Catholic**
- he favoured Roman Catholics and let them become army officers
- he did not work well with Parliament
- he arrested some bishops of the Church of England
- many feared he wanted to make England Catholic again
- at first, people expected a Protestant successor because his two daughters
  were Protestant
- then James's wife had a son
- now it looked likely that the next monarch would also be Catholic

## The Glorious Revolution
- James II's elder daughter **Mary** was married to **William of Orange**
  - Protestant ruler of the **Netherlands**
- **1688**: leading Protestants invited William to invade England
- William arrived and met no resistance
- James fled to **France**
- William took the throne as:
  - **William III** in England, Wales and Ireland
  - **William II** in Scotland
- William ruled jointly with **Mary**
- this became known as the **Glorious Revolution**
- called "glorious" because:
  - there was no fighting in England
  - it guaranteed the power of **Parliament**
  - it ended the danger of a monarch ruling alone as he wished

### Ireland / Scotland / Jacobites
- James II tried to regain the throne and invaded **Ireland** with French help
- **1690**: William defeated James at the **Battle of the Boyne**
- William re-conquered Ireland
- James fled back to France
- many restrictions were placed on the Roman Catholic Church in Ireland
- Irish Catholics were excluded from government
- there was also support for James in **Scotland**
- a rebellion for James was defeated at **Killiecrankie**
- all Scottish clans had to swear an oath accepting William as king
- the **MacDonalds of Glencoe** were late in taking the oath and were killed
- this massacre caused distrust of the new government
- supporters of James were called **Jacobites**
