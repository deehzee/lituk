# UK Government, the Law and Your Role — Respecting the Law

## Core idea
- one of the most important responsibilities in the UK is to **know and obey the law**
- all residents, whatever their background, are expected to comply with UK law
- something allowed in another country may still be **illegal in the UK**
- people who do not respect the law should not expect to become permanent residents
- the law affects everyday life, including:
  - personal affairs
  - business affairs

## The law in the UK
- everyone in the UK is treated **equally under the law**
- the law applies in the same way to everyone, regardless of who they are or
  where they come from

### Two main types of law
- **criminal law**
  - deals with crimes
  - usually investigated by police or another authority
  - punished by the courts
- **civil law**
  - settles disputes between individuals or groups

## Examples of criminal law
- **carrying a weapon**
  - illegal, even for self-defence
  - includes guns, knives, or anything adapted to cause injury
- **drugs**
  - buying or selling drugs such as heroin, cocaine, ecstasy and cannabis is illegal
- **racial crime**
  - illegal to harass, alarm or distress someone because of religion or ethnic origin
- **selling tobacco**
  - illegal to sell tobacco to anyone under **18**
- **smoking in public places**
  - illegal in almost all enclosed public places
- **buying alcohol for under-18s**
  - illegal to sell alcohol to under-18s
  - illegal to buy alcohol for under-18s
  - exception: people aged **16 or over** can drink alcohol with a meal in a
    hotel or restaurant
- **drinking in public**
  - some places are alcohol-free zones
  - police can confiscate alcohol, move people on, fine or arrest them
- other crimes also include:
  - murder
  - theft
  - assault

## Examples of civil law
- **housing law**
  - disputes between landlords and tenants
  - e.g. repairs, eviction
- **consumer rights**
  - disputes over faulty goods or services
- **employment law**
  - wages
  - unfair dismissal
  - discrimination at work
- **debt**
  - cases where money is owed

## The police and their duties
- the police are responsible for:
  - **protecting life and property**
  - **keeping the peace**
  - **preventing and detecting crime**
- police are organised into separate police forces
- each is headed by a **Chief Constable**
- police forces are **independent of the government**

### Police and Crime Commissioners
- in **November 2012**, **Police and Crime Commissioners (PCCs)** were elected
  in England and Wales
- PCCs:
  - set local police priorities
  - set the local policing budget
  - appoint the Chief Constable

### Standards for police
- the police serve and protect **everyone**
- police officers must obey the law themselves
- they must not:
  - misuse authority
  - make false statements
  - be rude or abusive
  - commit racial discrimination
- corrupt officers or those who misuse authority are punished severely

### PCSOs
- police officers are supported by **Police Community Support Officers (PCSOs)**
- PCSOs usually:
  - patrol streets
  - work with the public
  - support officers at crime scenes and major events

### Helping the police / complaints
- everyone in the UK is expected to help police prevent and detect crime where possible
- if arrested, you must be told why
- you can seek **legal advice**
- complaints about the police can be made:
  - at a police station
  - to the **Chief Constable**
  - to an independent body
- independent complaints bodies mentioned:
  - **Independent Police Complaints Commission** (England and Wales)
  - **Police Complaints Commissioner for Scotland**
  - **Police Ombudsman for Northern Ireland**

## Terrorism and extremism
- the UK faces a range of terrorist threats
- the text identifies the most serious as:
  - **Al Qa'ida**
  - its affiliates
  - similar organisations
- the UK also faces other terrorism, including:
  - **Northern Ireland-related terrorism**
- terrorist groups try to:
  - radicalise people
  - recruit people
- public support for such groups is described as very low
- people should also be aware of **extremism**
  - defined here as vocal or active opposition to fundamental British values
  - includes religious extremism and far-right extremism
- if someone tries to persuade you to join an extremist or terrorist cause,
  you should contact the **local police**

## The judiciary
- **judges** are collectively called the **judiciary**
- judges are responsible for:
  - interpreting the law
  - making sure trials are fair
- the government cannot interfere with this

### Role of judges
- if judges find that the government has acted illegally:
  - the government must change its policy
  - or ask Parliament to change the law
- if a public body breaches someone's legal rights, judges can order it to:
  - change its practices
  - and/or pay compensation
- judges also decide disputes between:
  - members of the public
  - organisations
- these can involve:
  - contracts
  - property
  - employment rights
  - accidents

## Criminal courts

### Magistrates' Courts / Justice of the Peace Courts
- in **England, Wales and Northern Ireland**, most minor criminal cases go to a
  **Magistrates' Court**
- in **Scotland**, minor criminal cases go to a **Justice of the Peace Court**
- magistrates / JPs are usually:
  - members of the local community
  - unpaid
  - not legally qualified
  - trained and supported by a legal adviser
- they decide:
  - the **verdict**
  - the **sentence**
- in **Northern Ireland**, minor cases are heard by a legally qualified paid
  **District Judge** or **Deputy District Judge**

### Crown Courts / Sheriff Courts / High Court
- in **England, Wales and Northern Ireland**, serious offences go to a
  **Crown Court**
  - heard by a **judge and jury**
- in **Scotland**, serious cases go to a **Sheriff Court**
  - with a sheriff
  - or sheriff and jury
- the most serious Scottish cases, such as murder, go to the **High Court**

### Juries
- juries are made up of members of the public chosen at random from the
  **electoral register**
- jury size:
  - **12** in England, Wales and Northern Ireland
  - **15** in Scotland
- verdicts:
  - **guilty**
  - **not guilty**
  - in Scotland also **not proven**
- if the jury finds the defendant guilty, the **judge** decides the sentence

### Youth Courts
- in **England, Wales and Northern Ireland**, cases involving **10- to 17-year-olds**
  normally go to a **Youth Court**
- heard by:
  - up to 3 specially trained magistrates
  - or a District Judge
- the most serious cases go to the **Crown Court**
- parents or carers are expected to attend
- the public cannot attend Youth Courts
- the young person's name or photo cannot be published
- in **Scotland**, children are dealt with through the
  **Children's Hearings System**
- in **Northern Ireland**, there is a system of **youth conferencing**

## Civil courts

### County Courts / Sheriff Courts / higher civil courts
- **County Courts** deal with many civil disputes, including:
  - money owed
  - personal injury
  - family matters
  - breach of contract
  - divorce
- in **Scotland**, most such matters go to the **Sheriff Court**
- more serious civil cases go to:
  - the **High Court** in England, Wales and Northern Ireland
  - the **Court of Session** in Edinburgh for Scotland

### Small claims procedure
- the **small claims procedure** is an informal way to settle minor disputes
  without much time or legal cost
- limits:
  - less than **£10,000** in **England and Wales**
  - less than **£5,000** in **Scotland and Northern Ireland**
- small claims hearings are held in an ordinary room before a judge
- both sides usually sit around a table
- small claims can also be started online

## Legal advice

### Solicitors
- **solicitors** are trained lawyers
- they:
  - give legal advice
  - act for clients
  - represent clients in court
- there are solicitor offices across the UK
- it is important to:
  - check the solicitor's area of specialism
  - check they have the right experience
  - ask early how much the case is likely to cost
- solicitor fees are usually based on the **time spent** on the case
- help finding solicitors can come from:
  - **Citizens Advice**
  - the relevant **Law Society**
