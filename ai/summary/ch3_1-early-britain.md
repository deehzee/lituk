# Early Britain

## Stone Age
- first people in Britain were **hunter-gatherers** in the **Stone Age**
- Britain was connected to Europe by a **land bridge**
- people moved in and out following herds of **deer** and **horses**
- about **10,000 years ago**: Britain became permanently separated from the
  continent by the **Channel**

### First farmers / monuments
- about **6,000 years ago**: first farmers arrived in Britain
- they probably came from **south-east Europe**
- they built:
  - houses
  - tombs
  - monuments
- important sites:
  - **Stonehenge** in **Wiltshire**
    - probably used for seasonal ceremonies
  - **Skara Brae** in **Orkney**
    - best-preserved prehistoric village in northern Europe
    - shows how people lived near the end of the Stone Age

## Bronze Age
- about **4,000 years ago**: people learned to make **bronze**
- this period is called the **Bronze Age**
- people:
  - lived in **roundhouses**
  - buried their dead in **round barrows**
- Bronze Age people were skilled metalworkers
  - made tools, ornaments and weapons in bronze and gold

## Iron Age
- after the Bronze Age came the **Iron Age**
- people learned to make tools and weapons from **iron**
- they still lived in roundhouses
- settlements became larger
- some sites were defended as **hill forts**
  - example: **Maiden Castle** in **Dorset**
- most people were:
  - farmers
  - craft workers
  - warriors
- their language was part of the **Celtic** language family
  - related languages are still spoken in parts of **Wales, Scotland and Ireland**
- Iron Age society had a sophisticated culture and economy
- they made the **first coins minted in Britain**
  - some had names of Iron Age kings on them
- this marks the **beginning of British history**

## The Romans

### Roman invasions
- **55 BC**: **Julius Caesar** tried to invade Britain
  - failed
- Britain remained outside the Roman Empire for nearly **100 years**
- **AD 43**: **Emperor Claudius** led a new invasion
  - this time the Romans occupied almost all of Britain
- some British tribes resisted
- one famous leader was **Boudicca**
  - queen of the **Iceni** in eastern England
  - there is a statue of her on **Westminster Bridge** in London

### Scotland / Hadrian's Wall
- the Romans never conquered what is now **Scotland**
- **Hadrian** built a wall in the north of England to keep out the **Picts**
  - ancestors of the Scottish people
- forts on Hadrian's Wall included:
  - **Housesteads**
  - **Vindolanda**
- Hadrian's Wall is now a **UNESCO World Heritage Site**

### Roman impact
- the Romans stayed in Britain for about **400 years**
- they:
  - built roads
  - built public buildings
  - created a structure of law
  - introduced new plants and animals
- in the **3rd and 4th centuries AD**, the first **Christian communities**
  appeared in Britain

## The Anglo-Saxons

### Arrival / kingdoms
- **AD 410**: the Roman army left Britain and never returned
- Britain was invaded by tribes from northern Europe:
  - **Jutes**
  - **Angles**
  - **Saxons**
- their languages became the basis of **modern English**
- by about **AD 600**, **Anglo-Saxon kingdoms** had been established
  - mainly in what is now **England**
- **Sutton Hoo** in **Suffolk** was the burial place of one of their kings
  - buried with treasure and armour
  - placed in a ship
  - covered by a mound of earth
- parts of western Britain, including much of **Wales**, and **Scotland**
  stayed outside Anglo-Saxon rule

### Christianity
- the Anglo-Saxons were not Christians at first
- missionaries later came to preach Christianity
- missionaries from **Ireland** spread Christianity in the north
  - **St Patrick**
    - later became the patron saint of Ireland
  - **St Columba**
    - founded a monastery on **Iona**
- **St Augustine** led missionaries from **Rome**
  - spread Christianity in the south
  - became the first **Archbishop of Canterbury**

## The Vikings
- the Vikings came from **Denmark** and **Norway**
- **AD 789**: first Viking raids on coastal towns
  - they took goods and slaves
- later they settled in the east of England and in Scotland
- Anglo-Saxon kingdoms united under **King Alfred the Great**
  - he defeated the Vikings
- many Vikings stayed in Britain, especially in eastern and northern England
  - this area was called the **Danelaw**
- place names such as **Grimsby** and **Scunthorpe** come from Viking languages
- Viking settlers mixed with local people
- some converted to **Christianity**

### Later rulers / Scotland
- Anglo-Saxon kings continued ruling England
  - except for a short period of **Danish kings**
- the first Danish king was **Cnut** / **Canute**
- in the north, Viking attacks encouraged people to unite under
  **Kenneth MacAlpin**
- the term **Scotland** began to be used for that country

## The Norman Conquest
- **1066**: **William**, Duke of **Normandy**, invaded England
- at the **Battle of Hastings**, he defeated **Harold**, the Saxon king
- Harold was killed
- William became king of England as **William the Conqueror**
- the battle is shown in the **Bayeux Tapestry**

### Effects of the Norman Conquest
- it was the **last successful foreign invasion of England**
- it changed government and social structures in England
- **Norman French**, the language of the ruling class,
  influenced the development of English
- the Normans first conquered **Wales**, but the Welsh later won land back
- the Normans fought the Scots on the border
  - they took some border land
  - but did **not** invade Scotland

### Domesday Book
- William ordered a huge survey of England
- it listed:
  - towns and villages
  - who lived there
  - who owned the land
  - what animals they owned
- this survey was called the **Domesday Book**
- it still exists today
- it gives a picture of life in England just after the Norman Conquest
