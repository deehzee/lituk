# Early Britain in 6 Scenes

## 1) The island breaks away
Picture a cold land full of deer and horses. The first people in Britain are **hunter-gatherers** in the **Stone Age**, moving with the animals they hunt. For a long time Britain is still connected to Europe by a land bridge, until it becomes permanently separated by the Channel about **10,000 years ago**. Then, about **6,000 years ago**, the first **farmers** arrive, settle down, and build monuments such as **Stonehenge**.

## 2) Metal, forts, and Celtic Britain
Now the camera jumps forward. Around **4,000 years ago**, people learn to make **bronze**: this is the **Bronze Age**. After that comes the **Iron Age**: people still live in roundhouses, gather in larger settlements, defend themselves with **hill forts**, speak **Celtic** languages, and make the **first coins minted in Britain**. Britain is no longer just a wild place; it already has warriors, craft workers, rulers, and organised communities.

## 3) Rome arrives and draws a line
A huge empire now looks across the sea. **Julius Caesar** invades in **55 BC**, but fails. Nearly 100 years later, in **AD 43**, Emperor **Claudius** invades successfully; the Romans occupy almost all of Britain, though tribal leaders such as **Boudicca**, queen of the **Iceni**, resist them. The Romans cannot fully conquer what is now Scotland, so Emperor **Hadrian** builds **Hadrian’s Wall** in the north of England to keep out the **Picts**.

## 4) Rome leaves, and new peoples rush in
The Romans stay about **400 years**. They build roads and public buildings, create a structure of law, introduce new plants and animals, and the first Christian communities appear in the **3rd and 4th centuries AD**. Then, in **AD 410**, the Roman army leaves Britain and never returns. Into the empty space come the **Jutes, Angles, and Saxons** from northern Europe; their languages become the basis of **modern English**, and by about **AD 600** Anglo-Saxon kingdoms are established, mainly in what is now England.

## 5) The sea-wolves
Now longships appear through mist. The **Vikings** come from **Denmark and Norway**, first raiding Britain in **AD 789**, taking goods and slaves from coastal towns. But they do not only raid; many stay, settling in the east and north of England and in Scotland. The English kingdoms unite under **King Alfred the Great** to defeat them, yet many Vikings remain in the **Danelaw**, and there is even a short period of **Danish kings**, the first being **Cnut (Canute)**.

## 6) 1066: one battle changes England
At last comes the most important scene. In **1066**, **William, Duke of Normandy**, from northern France, invades and defeats **Harold**, the Saxon king of England, at the **Battle of Hastings**; Harold is killed. William becomes **William the Conqueror**. This **Norman Conquest** is the **last successful foreign invasion of England**, brings major changes in government and society, makes **Norman French** the language of the ruling class, and leads to the **Domesday Book**, the great survey of England’s towns, villages, land, people, and animals.

## The memory chain

Remember it as:

**Hunters -> Farmers -> Bronze/Iron Age -> Romans -> Anglo-Saxons -> Vikings -> Normans**

And the anchor dates are:

- **10,000 years ago** Britain separates
- **6,000 years ago** first farmers
- **4,000 years ago** Bronze Age
- **55 BC** Caesar fails
- **AD 43** Claudius succeeds
- **AD 410** Romans leave
- **AD 600** Anglo-Saxon kingdoms established
- **AD 789** Vikings first raid
- **1066** William defeats Harold at Hastings

## Core idea

**Britain keeps changing hands, but each wave leaves something behind** — monuments from the Stone Age, metalworking and coins from Bronze/Iron Age Britain, roads and law from Rome, language from the Anglo-Saxons, place names and the Danelaw from the Vikings, and a new ruling class plus the Domesday Book from the Normans.
